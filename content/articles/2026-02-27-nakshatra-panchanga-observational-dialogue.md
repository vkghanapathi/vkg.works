abstract: A verbatim dialogue-based reconstruction documenting a sustained inquiry
  into nakshatra observation, sidereal anchoring, and the logical foundations of Panchanga-based
  ritual timekeeping. The work addresses the distinction between stellar references
  and tropical definitions, explores the relationship between Vedic nakshatras and
  Greco-Babylonian zodiacs, and examines how methodological drift in calendrical systems
  affects the transmission and reliability of traditional ritual timing. It also demonstrates
  the use of modern computational tools (Stellarium) for verification of classical
  naked-eye astronomical observation.
preamble: This work is situated within the Vedāṅga Jyotiṣa tradition—the mathematical
  astronomy and calendrical science codified as an auxiliary discipline to the Vedas—and
  specifically addresses the practical and theoretical foundations of Pañchāṅga (Hindu
  calendrical) systems. The Vedāṅga Jyotiṣa emphasizes sidereal observation and stellar
  anchoring for the correct timing of Vedic rituals and sacrifices. The dialogue explores
  tensions between the older nakshatra system of 27 lunar asterisms and the later
  adoption of the 12-sign zodiac, examining how these methodologies converge and diverge,
  and what is at stake when definitions shift from sidereal (star-based) to tropical
  (seasonal) reference frames.
